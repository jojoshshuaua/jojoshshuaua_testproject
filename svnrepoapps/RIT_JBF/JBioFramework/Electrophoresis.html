Electrophoresis

Separation based on size

Proteins are subsequently separated on the basis of their molecular mass using sodium dodecyl sulphate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE). Proteins resolved in the first dimension are applied to a second gel and separated perpendicularly to the first dimension.

Detergents

Before the second separation proteins are treated with the detergent sodium dodecyl sulphate. This coats all proteins in proportion to their mass and ensures they all have a large negative charge. The proteins are then applied to a gel matrix which sieves them on the basis of their size when an electric current is applied. Upon electrophoresis proteins move through the gel towards the anode. The rate at which they move is inversely proportional to their molecular mass, larger proteins feeling more resistance and hence taking longer to move.

SDS is chosen because it binds to most proteins in a constant fashion (about 1.4 grams of SDS per gram of protein) and also masks any charge of the protein by forming these large anionic complexes. SDS also disrupts any hydrogen bonds, blocks many hydrophobic interactions and partially unfolds the protein molecules minimising differences based on secondary or tertiary structure. A reducing agent such as beta-mercapthoethanol or dithiothreitol is also often included in order to totally unfold proteins and detach subunits to ensure a separation based exclusively on molecular weight.

Different variations

There are many different methods of performing SDS-PAGE based on whether analysis is required in a native or denatured state and the size of the molecules being analysed. The most common method is that of Laemmli, while the method of Schagger and von Jagow is popular for polypeptides of 10 kDa or less.

Limitations

Two-dimensional gel electrophoresis (2DE) is an extremely efficient process, able to resolve many thousands of proteins, but it is does suffer from a series of limitations.

Proteins analysed by 2DE are not easily recoverable. It is possible to remove them from the gel using electroblotting but this is a difficult and imprecise technique. It is usually easier to digest the proteins within the gel matrix using a protease enzyme such as trypsin.

Analyses performed by gels are not particularly quantitative due to the differing amounts proteins take up the staining reagents. Careful use of calibrated standards does allow some quantification, but even this is somewhat imprecise.

2DE takes a long time to perform. Each of the individual stages requires quite a long time to run, and much user intervention is required. This makes 2DE an expensive technique in terms of worker hours.

Proteins often react with the gel matrix. This leads to differing amounts of acrylamide-protein polymers which makes identify proteins masses difficult. The interactions also mean that mass spectrometry is complicated, especially when combined with the fact that the proteins are hard to extract from the gel.

Performing 2DE is very much an art, requiring much experimentation to find the correct conditions for sample preparation, focusing times etc. If these are not optimised then artifacts can easily be introduced into the 2D pattern.
